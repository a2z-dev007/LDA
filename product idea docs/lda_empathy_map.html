Empathy Map Canvas — Let's Date Again
Designed for The Committed Partner (26–42, urban India)
Designed by LDA Founding Team
Version 1.0
Goal
To build a daily 10-minute personal habit that helps a committed individual communicate better, feel more intentional about their relationship, and bridge the gap between what they feel and what they say — before the silence becomes the distance.
1
Who are we empathising with?
Person · situation · role
  • Priya or Arjun — urban professional, 28–40 years old, committed for 2–7 years. Married or in a serious relationship.
  • Lives in Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru, Hyderabad or Pune. Both partners working full-time.
  • Relationship is not in crisis. It is functional, affectionate, and quietly not what it used to be.
  • They are the person in the couple who feels something first and hasn't said it yet. The first mover.
  • Emotionally self-aware enough to know something is off. Not sure how to name it. Not ready to call it a problem.
  • Possibly a new parent, recently relocated, or navigating a big life transition that took priority over the relationship.
2
What do they need to do?
Jobs · decisions · success signals
  • Find a way to reconnect with their partner without making it feel like a serious conversation or a crisis intervention.
  • Build a habit of checking in with themselves about the relationship — something small, daily, not overwhelming.
  • Learn to identify and name what they are feeling before they can express it.
  • Decide whether to show the app to their partner or start alone first — the solo entry point removes this pressure.
  • Feel like they are doing something active for their relationship rather than hoping things improve on their own.
  • Success looks like: completing 5 days, having at least one conversation they had been avoiding, feeling less like they are drifting and more like they are choosing.
7
What do they think and feel?
Inner world — pains and gains
Pains — fears, frustrations, anxieties
  • They feel something is off but cannot name it, so they stay silent. The silence compounds.
  • Fear that bringing it up will start a fight or make their partner feel blamed.
  • Anxiety that if they say the wrong thing it will make things worse, not better.
  • Quiet resentment that they always seem to be the one who notices and the one who tries.
  • Worry that they have drifted too far from who they used to be as a couple.
  • Shame about not being more proactive earlier — feeling like they let things get to this point.
  • Fear of therapy's stigma — "are we that couple now?"
  • Loneliness that is strange because the other person is right there.
Gains — wants, needs, hopes, dreams
  • To feel like their partner actually sees them — not just the version they are used to.
  • To have one honest conversation that clears the air without becoming an argument.
  • To feel intentional in their relationship again — like they are choosing it, not just maintaining it.
  • A small daily ritual that is theirs — private, meaningful, not another notification.
  • To understand themselves better in the context of this relationship.
  • The confidence that they are doing something rather than hoping things get better on their own.
  • A way to invite their partner in at the right moment — when they have something real to show.
  • A relationship they are proud of — still curious about each other at year 10.
Other motivating thoughts: "I don't want to wait until it's bad enough to need therapy." · "I used to be better at this." · "We don't talk the way we used to." · "I love them — I just don't know how to say what I mean anymore."
3
What do they see?
Marketplace · environment · influences
  • Friends who appear to have perfectly curated relationships on Instagram — and suspect most of it is performance.
  • Couples around them who either seem effortlessly happy or are quietly unhappy — no visible middle ground.
  • Dating apps and matrimonial apps everywhere — nothing built for people who already have a partner.
  • Therapy being normalised for individuals but still carrying stigma for couples ("couples therapy = we're in trouble").
  • Content about communication, love languages, attachment styles flooding their social feeds — they consume it but find it abstract.
  • Their own relationship in flashes — a good weekend, a distant weeknight, a small kindness, a missed moment.
  • No app or product that speaks directly to them — the person in a committed relationship who wants to do better proactively.
4
What do they say?
Quotes · language · expressions
  • "We're fine. Just busy."
  • "I feel like we don't really talk anymore — not properly."
  • "I don't want to make it a big thing. It's probably nothing."
  • "I love them. I just don't know how to say what I actually mean."
  • "We used to be so different together."
  • "I'm not unhappy — but I remember when this felt like more."
  • "I don't need therapy. I just need us to be more present."
  • "Every time I try to bring something up it becomes an argument."
  • "I wish there was some way to just… restart without making it a whole conversation."
5
What do they do?
Observed behaviours · actions · patterns
  • Scrolls Instagram at night instead of talking. Both phones out at dinner.
  • Saves relationship content — articles, reels, carousels — but rarely applies it.
  • Occasionally sends their partner a meme or article that says what they cannot say directly.
  • Plans date nights that get postponed because life comes first.
  • Has the same low-level argument repeatedly without resolving the thing underneath it.
  • Writes in a diary or journaling app privately — but never bridges it to the relationship.
  • Googles things like "how to talk to your partner about feeling disconnected" at 11pm.
  • Notices the small gestures their partner makes but rarely says so out loud.
  • Waits for the "right moment" to say something important. The moment does not come.
6
What do they hear?
Friends · colleagues · second-hand
  • From friends: "Every couple goes through phases. It'll pass." — which is reassuring but does nothing.
  • From colleagues: "Marriage is hard work" — normalises the difficulty without offering a way through.
  • From social media: "Learn your love language" / "Communicate more" — useful frameworks, no practical entry point.
  • From parents: "In our time we just got on with it." — makes them feel like wanting more is a weakness.
  • From culture broadly: relationships that need work are broken. Relationships that are working need no attention. Both false.
  • Occasionally from a brave friend: "We started couples therapy and it changed everything" — intriguing but feels extreme for where they are.
  • From the internet: relationship content that is either crisis-level advice or superficial listicles. Nothing for the middle.
